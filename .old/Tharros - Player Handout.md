# **Welcome to Tharros**

**An Island of Promise, Mystery, and Opportunity**

## “A future worth forging. A past worth unearthing. A balance worth protecting.”

Tharros is a land of possibility—and peril. Forgotten ruins, strange storms, and whispering forests meet newly laid roads, fresh hopes, and firelit temples.

People from across the mainland and beyond have made their way here: settlers, scholars, visionaries, and exiles. Some come chasing legends. Others come running from them.

But none leave unchanged.

## On the Relics – Their Purpose and Origin

*From “Reflections on the Relics of Tharros” by Sera Vindell, Independent Scholar*

Relics defy easy classification. Some believe they are tools left behind by a vanished civilization—complex machines that regulate the world’s laws in ways we can no longer understand. Others argue they are not artifacts at all, but parts of the world itself, like veins in stone or bones beneath the sea.

Their effects vary: some bolster the arcane, others suppress it. Some spawn monsters, others seem to repel them. There are those who say each Relic has a specific function, part of a larger system we’ve barely glimpsed. Others suggest their behavior is shaped by proximity, intention, or even belief.

The prevailing scholarly stance, especially within the Brotherhood, is that the Relics are technological in nature—machines with misunderstood programming. The Church is more divided, sometimes viewing them as divine artifacts, sometimes as dangerous distractions from true faith.

Whether relics are remnants, guardians, curses, or seeds—we do not know. What is clear is this: they change the world around them. And perhaps us as well.
<!-- CATEGORY: LORE -->## On the Ancestors – Builders, Beasts, or Beheld?

*From “The Vanished Age” – Brotherhood Archives, Collated Oral Traditions*

Many tales speak of the ones who came before—“the Ancestors,” as they are often called. Some believe they were like us but more advanced, their mastery over creation so refined it appeared to us as magic. Others claim they were something else entirely: creatures of light, thought, or will, barely resembling mortals at all.

A few radical voices propose that the Ancestors were never a single people, but a succession of civilizations, each rising and falling beneath the weight of their own ambitions. The remnants of their existence—Relics, buried cities, altered landscapes—may belong to different hands across vast spans of time.

Among the most contested ideas is whether the Ancestors vanished of their own accord, were destroyed in catastrophe, or simply evolved into something we would no longer recognize.

In common circles, the Ancestors are viewed as noble forebears whose wisdom was lost to time. But some whisper that they tampered with things better left untouched—and that we now walk in the echoes of their failure.
<!-- CATEGORY: LORE, MECHANIC -->## On the Reckonings – Natural Disaster or Judgment?

*From “Storms of Thought: A Study of the Reckonings” by Brannis Heth, Veiled Observer*

The Reckonings are not like other storms. They come suddenly, with little regard for season or geography. Their impact is often devastating, and yet no pattern has been reliably mapped. Some settlements suffer repeatedly. Others, never.

The Brotherhood theorizes that Reckonings are reactive events—environmental corrections triggered by interference with Relics, deep excavations, or magical excess. The Assembly sees them as proof that the land itself resists exploitation, its balance disturbed by greed and ignorance.

Some theologians believe the Reckonings are divine punishment—warnings sent from the Eternal Flame or forgotten gods, though they rarely agree on what sin is being punished.

The Syndicate dismisses spiritual explanations, viewing them as unfortunate but manageable risks of frontier expansion. Yet even their reports quietly note the strange timing and concentrated damage near Relic sites.

Most people, in truth, do not know what causes the Reckonings. They only know that when they come, they must flee—and hope they are not seen.
<!-- CATEGORY: LORE, MECHANIC -->## On Magic – Natural Force or Mirrored Dream?

*From “Of Flame and Thought” – Comparative Arcanology Lecture, Ashlight University*

Magic is everywhere, but it is not understood. Some liken it to wind or heat—a natural force, invisible but real, accessible with the right methods. Others call it a dialogue between the caster and the world, where intention reshapes reality. A few suggest it is neither: a ghost of the Relics’ presence, sustained only by their lingering influence.

Near powerful Relics, magic intensifies—but becomes unstable. Some see this as proof that the Relics power magic. Others say they simply amplify what is already within the caster.

There are also theories that magic is a form of language or belief—that what we call a “spell” is a ritualized thought, granted substance by ancient agreements, or even sentience beyond our ken.

The Church acknowledges magic as real, but dangerous. It teaches that true miracles come only from the Flame, and other forms of power are unclean or suspect.

In the end, magic may be both tool and mirror—shaped by will, altered by fear, and never entirely in our grasp.

<!-- DUPLICATE: Same content exists in "Tharros - GM Notes.md" under "Divinity" section -->## On Divinity – Flame, Fragments, or Fabrication?

*From “Many Faces, One Fire?” – Disputed Scroll of the Third Illumination*

There are gods—this much most people believe. But whether they are many or one, mortal or eternal, remains ever debated.

The Church of the Eternal Flame teaches that all divine power is but one light, refracted through different names and symbols. According to them, the Eternal Flame is both origin and destiny—the ordered fire from which creation rose and to which it must return.

Other faiths persist, especially in the margins: river gods, stone spirits, ancestral whispers. Some hold that the Relics themselves are divine—or at least touched by divinity.

Skeptics point to the inconsistencies. If gods are real, why are they silent? Why do miracles vary? Some suggest the gods are dead. Others that they never were, and belief alone sustains the rites and visions.

Still, most cling to some version of faith. Not always because it explains, but because it comforts. Whether the Flame is truth or symbol, it continues to burn.

## On Flying

*“To fly is to forget the pact of flesh and stone.”* — Saying among the Hollow Priests

It begins subtly. The lift feels heavier than it should. Wind patterns, even indoors, pull in strange counter-directions. The air presses against you—gently at first, like hands guiding you back to the ground. The longer you persist, the stranger it becomes.

Some say the Island was not meant to be seen from above. That flight in its skies, or even within its ancient places, offends something vast and long-slumbering. Others claim the Relics twist the very currents of magic and breath, warping perception, then balance, then reason.

First comes the disorientation: vertical lines curve, floors ripple, distances lie. Then the nausea. Then a low ringing in your ears, as if the world is warning you in a voice just too deep to hear.

Few fly far. Fewer fly again.

And those who do—those who return from prolonged time aloft—speak less of what they saw than of what they *felt*. Like being watched. Like gravity wasn’t a force, but a judgment.
<!-- CATEGORY: LORE, LOCATION -->## On Those Who Came Before

Tharros has not always been untouched.

Though the Ancestors left behind their impossible Relics and silent ruins, more recent settlers have also tried to claim the island. Ships have landed. Villages have been built. Expeditions have been mapped and lost.

The oldest of the current settlers sometimes speak of **“The Bone Ring”**, a half-buried circle of stone foundations far inland—said to be all that remains of a mining town established thirty years ago and gone within three. Another tale tells of the **Fledges**, a utopian farming commune that vanished between two Reckonings.

Ask the Assembly, and they will tell you: *the land does not forget its scars*.

The Syndicate speaks little of these past attempts, though some of their workers wear rings and amulets clearly older than the current wave.

The Brotherhood, of course, claims these lost settlements are **evidence**—data points, not tragedies.

The Church calls them **“unprepared hearts”**—those who came without divine order to guide them.

And the commonfolk? Some say they see flickering lights in places long abandoned. That voices whisper in the old wells. That some footsteps never fade.

## On the Church

*From “On Flame and Structure” – a forbidden commentary by an unnamed cleric, now preserved only in fragments whispered among exiles and dissenters.*

The Church of the Eternal Flame claims unity above all. One Flame. One truth. One path to salvation. To many, it offers comfort in a chaotic world—ritual, hierarchy, and answers to unanswerable things.

It teaches that all gods are reflections of the Flame, that miracles are but glimpses of divine order, and that true virtue lies in discipline, clarity, and purpose. Even when it tolerates other religions, it demands they align with its tenets: the Flame is supreme, even when hidden.

Some see this as wisdom. Others see it as conquest cloaked in theology.

On the mainland, the Church is vast, old, and deeply enmeshed with governance. Here, on Tharros, it is newer, smaller, but still influential. Officially, it mediates disputes and offers sanctuary. Unofficially, it positions itself as the sole moral compass—implicitly or explicitly.

Critics suggest that the Church’s true goal is not salvation but *control*. That it uses faith to mold society into manageable forms. That its centralization is less about preserving truth and more about ensuring obedience.

And yet... when the monsters come, it is often Church clerics who stand first. When plagues strike, it is their candles that still burn.

Perhaps the Church is both shepherd and jailer. Perhaps that is the price of fire—it warms, and it scars.

## The Mainland

*"They say the Mainland stretches endlessly—lands of spires and smoke, of banners and borders, of old wars and older laws. It is where most come from, and where most plan to return. At least, at first."*

For most, **the Mainland** is simply *home*. Whether a towering city-state, a quiet border village, or a nomadic caravan roaming forgotten trails, it is the place the people of Tharros left behind. The reasons vary: **hope, exile, coin, curiosity, faith**, or simply the pull of the unknown.

What unites the Mainland is its **distance**—not just in miles, but in meaning. News rarely travels back and forth. Ships come, but not often, and never on a schedule. The world inland marches on, forgetting Tharros even as some feel drawn to it.

* Some say the Mainland is ruled by **competing empires**, their wars long and cold.

* Others speak of **great academies**, where magic is studied like science—and feared just as much.

* The **Church of the Eternal Flame** claims dominion there, but not everyone kneels.

* There are cities where **elves, dwarves, orcs, and humans** mingle freely—and regions where such coexistence is unthinkable.

* There are places rich with magic, and others where it's outlawed or broken.

But no single truth defines the Mainland. Let your character’s memory of it be your own.

*"They say the world is wide, but the Island is deep. You don’t measure Tharros in leagues, you measure it in what it changes in you."*

### **1\. 📜 On the Nature of the Relics**

*Excerpt from “The Bloom and the Blade,” a tract attributed to the hermit-scholar known only as Fenlight.*

We call them *Relics* because we do not understand them. A child calls a god a star, and a star a god, and worships both with the same candle.

The Relics are neither divine nor mechanical, neither sacred nor mundane. They are *persistent*. That is the only provable quality. They do not decay. They do not forget their purpose. Even when shattered, the air hums with the memory of their function.

Some scholars argue they are tools—devices left behind by the Ancestors. Others whisper they are beings in dormancy, and the hum is a heartbeat. My belief is more terrible: the Relics are not *from* the Ancestors. The Relics *created* them.

We are not the builders. We are the results.

<!-- CATEGORY: LORE, MECHANIC -->### **3\. 🌪️ From “Cycles and Silence: A Reckoning Primer”**

*Recovered pages from a Brotherhood research diary, classified until breach of ethics revealed misuse of fieldworkers.*

A Reckoning is not a natural storm. Though it mimics weather, it follows no cycle, no pressure front. It responds only to intent and disruption.

In three observed cases, mining operations triggered Reckonings within seven days of major excavation. In two, the Reckoning struck within *hours*.

Patterns emerge:

* Relic disturbance (especially power cores) is a high correlation factor.

* Large-scale resource extraction increases frequency.

* Ritual activity near ancient sites seems to delay events.

Our working theory is that the Reckonings are environmental reflexes. Immune responses. The island corrects imbalances.

The question remains: what does it consider “healthy”?

<!-- CATEGORY: LORE, ITEM, MECHANIC -->## Magic and Relics: Debates and Hypotheses

### **I. Why Do Relics Amplify or Suppress Magic?**

**Hypothesis A: Relics Are Magical Wellsprings**

*“Magic is a residual field, and Relics are its sources. The ancients either built them to channel this field—or they formed around it, like coral around a sunken ship. The more intact a Relic, the more magic it emits. Those that nullify magic are either exhausted or built to contain the force instead of release it.”*  
 —Excerpt from *The Flow Beneath*, banned Brotherhood scroll, unknown author

**Hypothesis B: Relics Refract Consciousness, Not Power**

*“Magic is will given form. The presence of a Relic warps will like a lens warps light. That’s why spells twist near them. They don’t amplify anything—they distort intent. It’s not the Relic that is powerful. It’s the caster who becomes unstable.”*  
 —Notes from a half-burned journal found in a null zone ruin

**Hypothesis C: Relics Are Interference**

*“Relics are foreign objects to this world. Magic, being a native current of life, rejects or reacts to them violently. The amplification is not natural—it is friction. What you’re feeling is not power. It’s backlash.”*  
 —Statement recorded during a tribunal trial of a rogue Flame-born priest, 27 years ago

### **II. Why Do Some Relics Attract Monsters?**

**Hypothesis A: Monsters Are Drawn to the Heat**

*“Monsters are like moths. They feel the pulse of Relics in ways we cannot, and they fly to it. Not out of malice—but instinct. We think they’re evil because they gather where we die, but they were there first. Maybe we’re the infection.”*  
 —From a tract written by a recluse near the Weeping Tower, signed only “—S.”

**Hypothesis B: Relics Produce Monsters**

*“It’s not that monsters go to the Relics. They come from them. The worse the ruin, the stranger the creature. It’s not a coincidence. It’s birth. The Relics are wombs—or dumps. What’s worse is that they may not be done.”*  
 —Remnant Brotherhood internal memorandum (heavily redacted)

**Hypothesis C: Monsters and Magic Are the Same Thing**

*“A spell and a monster are both expressions of will. The difference is in control. A spell ends. A monster doesn’t. When magic near a Relic gets wild enough, it stabilizes. Into form. Into hunger. Into teeth.”*  
 —Spoken aloud by an Assembly radical before detonating a glyph chamber

**Hypothesis A: Stable Relics Emit Harmonics**

### **IV. Why Do Some Relics Nullify Magic?**

**Hypothesis A: Relics As Sinks**

*“Some Relics are not fountains but drains. They absorb magic like water into sand. The ancients may have built them to seal something—maybe magic itself. Maybe themselves. Standing inside one is like shouting in a vacuum.”*  
 —Theory from the lost treatise *White Silence and Black Stone*

**Hypothesis B: Proximity Is an Overload**

*“Imagine pouring too much energy into a wire. It burns out. A null Relic doesn’t suppress magic. It overwhelms it until the weave collapses. You feel nothing, because your magic is dead before it leaves your mind.”*

### **V. Why Do Relics Affect Spells Differently?**

**Hypothesis A: Resonance Matching**

*“Every Relic vibrates at a frequency. Every school of magic has a tone. Some match. Some clash. That's why fire spells might amplify, and illusion spells might backfire, even in the same place.”*  
 —Personal theory of a wanderer known only as Dhalé

**Hypothesis B: Sentience**

*“The Relics choose. That’s the only pattern. They are not tools. They are judges. Cast a spell they approve of, and you flourish. Cast something they despise, and you burn.”*  
 —Inscribed on a stone tablet found at the foot of a half-melted statue

**Hypothesis C: It’s About Intent, Not Effect**

*“Two wizards cast the same spell. One believes. One doubts. One is kind. One cruel. The Relic responds to one, distorts the other. It doesn't care what you cast—it cares why.”*  
 —Fragment of a lost philosophical dialogue, attributed to "The Mouthless One"

### **VI. Why Do Cities Form Around Some Relics?**

**Hypothesis A: Safety**

*“The Relics that repel monsters give people peace. That alone is reason enough to build. Over time, the people forget what lies beneath. They build temples. Taverns. Families. They forget to ask why.”*  
 —Folk story told in the shade of Beacon Hill

**Hypothesis B: Power and Control**

*“Every settlement wants an edge. A Relic makes them strong. Or unique. Or rich. Even if it twists the land, even if it brings nightmares once a season, they won’t give it up. People are used to living next to volcanoes.”*  
 —Noted in a Syndicate logistics manual under “Strategic Asset Positioning”

**Hypothesis C: No One Chose to Settle There**

*“You don’t build near a Relic. You find yourself there. Wake up in a city that wasn’t there last year. The Relic calls people. Shapes them. Plants a seed. You don’t choose the place. It chooses you.”*  
 —Written in charcoal on a cellar wall during a fever plague; city abandoned six weeks later
